# NSF Annual Report Intake Questionnaire

Use this questionnaire after inspecting the active proposal folder and before drafting. Ask only questions that are not already answered by reliable local evidence. When possible, present inferred answers for confirmation.

Do not produce a final Research.gov-ready draft with `[TODO]`, `[VERIFY]`, or `[EDIT]` markers. If the user does not know an answer, ask whether the correct final treatment is "none", "not applicable", "unknown", or "omit this detail".

Before asking detailed questions, look for `nsf-report-context.md` in the active proposal folder. If it does not exist, create it from `references/project-context-template.md` or ask the user whether to create it. Use that file as the durable source of variables and annual facts.

## Start Here

Ask the user to answer in rough notes or bullets; polished prose is not required.

1. Should I create or update `nsf-report-context.md` for this project?
2. What reporting period should this annual report cover?
3. What award number, project title, PI, co-PIs, and institutions should appear in the report?
4. Were there any agency-approved changes to the project goals, scope, approach, key personnel, budget, or timeline during this period?
5. Are there files, notes, publication lists, CV entries, repositories, calendars, or prior drafts I should use before drafting?

## Goals and Milestones

1. Should the approved major goals remain unchanged from the proposal?
2. What milestones were planned for this reporting period?
3. Which milestones were completed, partially completed, deferred, or changed?
4. What approximate percent completion should be reported by major goal, thread, task, or milestone?
5. Were there significant changes in methods, approach, or project direction?

## Accomplishments

1. What were the main research activities this period?
2. What specific objectives were pursued?
3. What concrete results were produced, such as theorems, proofs, algorithms, code, models, simulations, experiments, benchmark results, datasets, or analyses?
4. Which results are significant, and why do they matter for intellectual merit?
5. Were there transformative outcomes, unexpected findings, negative results, delays, or lessons learned?
6. What work is in progress but not yet complete?
7. Which activities connect to each approved project thread or task?

## Products

1. What papers, preprints, conference papers, workshop papers, abstracts, posters, reports, or manuscripts were submitted, accepted, published, or prepared?
2. What software, repositories, datasets, models, benchmarks, protocols, websites, or other artifacts were created or updated?
3. Were any products publicly released? If yes, provide URLs, DOIs, repository names, or release status.
4. Were there patents, invention disclosures, licenses, or other technology-transfer products?

## People and Training

1. Who worked on the project during this reporting period? Include roles such as graduate student, undergraduate student, postdoc, faculty, staff, REU student, or collaborator.
2. Who was financially supported by the award?
3. What mentoring or training did each person receive?
4. How often did mentoring occur, such as weekly meetings, one-on-one advising, lab meetings, reading groups, code reviews, or writing feedback?
5. Did trainees attend conferences, workshops, seminars, summer schools, tutorials, or professional-development events?
6. Did trainees give talks, posters, demos, classroom presentations, or public presentations?
7. Were there undergraduate research, VIP, REU, classroom, curriculum, or course-project activities?
8. Are names appropriate to include, or should trainee descriptions be anonymized/generalized?

## Dissemination and Broader Impacts

1. How were results disseminated to communities of interest?
2. Which communities were reached, such as CPS, controls, robotics, hybrid systems, optimization, formal methods, education, industry, or the public?
3. Were there journal/conference submissions, invited talks, seminars, posters, workshops, tutorials, panels, software releases, websites, CPS-VO activity, ARCH-COMP activity, outreach, or classroom dissemination?
4. Did the project contribute to broader impacts through training, inclusion, undergraduate research, curriculum, public engagement, open-source tools, reproducibility, or safety-critical CPS applications?
5. Did planned outreach or dissemination activities not happen? If so, should they be omitted or described as planned for next period?

## Impact Page

Ask these when preparing the Research.gov Impact page. Short answers are enough.

1. What was the main impact on the project's principal discipline?
2. Did the work affect other fields? If yes, which ones and how?
3. What was the impact on students, trainees, or research workforce development?
4. Did the project affect teaching, courses, mentoring, classroom materials, online resources, or educational experiences?
5. Did the project create or improve physical infrastructure, such as hardware, facilities, instruments, labs, or testbeds?
6. Did the project create or improve institutional infrastructure, such as programs, practices, partnerships, or organizational capacity?
7. Did the project create or improve information infrastructure, such as code, datasets, repositories, data services, or preservation?
8. Was there any technology-transfer impact, including open-source use, public use, invention disclosures, patents, licenses, or commercialization?
9. Was there any impact on society beyond science and technology, such as safety, economic benefit, public welfare, policy, education, or environmental benefit?
10. What percentage of the award budget was spent in a foreign country during this reporting period? If none, say 0%.

## Data, Compliance, and Sharing

1. Were data, code, or materials generated that need to be described under the data management plan?
2. Were data or software shared publicly, shared on request, or held pending publication?
3. Were there human subjects, animal subjects, safety, export-control, proprietary, or privacy considerations?
4. Were there collaborations, subawards, or facilities used that should be acknowledged?

## Next Reporting Period

1. What are the planned research activities for the next reporting period?
2. What are the planned milestones, deliverables, publications, software/data releases, experiments, or demonstrations?
3. What training, mentoring, outreach, classroom, VIP, REU, or dissemination activities are planned?
4. What risks, dependencies, delays, or resource needs should be mentioned?
5. Are there any goals or plans that should be updated based on this year's progress?

## Final Confirmation

Before drafting final text, confirm:

1. There are no remaining unknown facts required for the Research.gov responses.
2. "None" and "not applicable" answers are intentional where used.
3. The report should be drafted as a clean Research.gov-ready response rather than a working draft with placeholders.
